{{ T('guide', 'h_scale', 'Where the air number comes from, and what it is not') }}

{{ T('guide', 'scale_1', "The readings come from India's CPCB monitoring stations, delivered through the WAQI feed. WAQI publishes its numbers on the United States EPA index, not India's — it moved every Indian station onto the US scale in January 2016, and says itself that its figures will therefore differ from India's own National AQI portal.") }}

{{ T('guide', 'scale_2', 'The two scales are far apart. 60 µg/m³ of PM2.5 is 100 "Satisfactory" on India\'s scale and about 154 "Unhealthy" on the US one. So this site converts: it turns the feed\'s US index values back into concentrations, then works out the Indian figure from those. That way the number you see here is the kind of number you see everywhere else in Delhi.') }}

{{ T('guide', 'scale_3', "What that number is not. It is worked out from PM2.5 and PM10 only. India's official method uses up to eight pollutants and needs at least three, so on a day when a gas like ozone is the worst thing in the air, the official figure would be higher than ours. The provenance panel under any answer shows both our number and WAQI's original, so you can see the difference for yourself.") }}
